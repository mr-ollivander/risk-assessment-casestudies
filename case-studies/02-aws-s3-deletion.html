Case Study 02 · Post-Incident Risk Assessment

Accidental Production Data Deletion
via AWS CLI Misfire

A retrospective risk assessment of a real-world incident — working backwards from what happened to identify what should have been in the risk register before it did.

AWS S3 · IAM · Data Loss
Overall Risk: High
Retrospective Assessment
⚠
This is a post-incident assessment Unlike a prospective risk assessment, this case study works backwards from a confirmed incident. The risk was real — and it materialized. The assessment identifies what controls should have existed before it did.
The Scenario
What happened

A SaaS company ran both staging and production environments under a single shared AWS account, separated by naming conventions only — no separate accounts, no VPC isolation, no enforced tagging policy.

A junior engineer deploying a new feature to staging ran the following command, intending to target a staging bucket:

aws s3 rm s3://company-data-prod --recursive

The command completed successfully. The production bucket contained 180GB of customer data exports accumulated over three years. There were no S3 versioning or replication policies. There were no automated backups. The data is permanently gone.

  • Customers are already raising tickets asking why their exports are missing
  • No technical control prevented the command from executing
  • The engineer had no malicious intent — this was an accidental misfire
The Thinking
How I reasoned through it
First call — this is not a risk assessment

The first thing I noted is that this situation is an active incident, not a prospective risk. Risk assessment is forward-looking. The moment that command completed, we crossed into incident response territory. Different process, different owner, different urgency.

Where risk assessment re-enters

Risk assessment still has a role — but retrospectively. The incident becomes evidence. It confirms a risk existed, tells us likelihood was real rather than theoretical, and gives precise information about which vulnerabilities actually enabled it. That's richer input to a risk register than any hypothetical scenario.

Accidental threat raises likelihood, not lowers it

My instinct was that a non-malicious actor means lower severity. Wrong. Accidental threats are often rated higher likelihood than malicious ones. A malicious actor needs motive, opportunity, and intent aligned simultaneously. An accidental actor just needs to make one mistake — mistype a bucket name, run a command in the wrong terminal tab. Those mistakes scale directly with how many people have access and how much pressure they're under to ship. When the only barrier between a destructive action and its execution is human accuracy rather than a technical control, likelihood is structurally elevated regardless of intent.

Refining the vulnerabilities

My first instinct was to call out network segmentation and tagging policy as separate vulnerabilities. I revised this. The engineer wasn't breaking through a network boundary — they were running a CLI command they were already technically authorized to run. The real vulnerability is IAM: a junior engineer had permissions to execute destructive commands against production resources when their role required no such access. Naming conventions and tagging are administrative mitigations that don't technically prevent the action. Only IAM does.

Three-dimensional impact

The impact isn't just data loss. It operates across three dimensions simultaneously: operational (180GB permanently gone, no recovery path), regulatory (data protection laws mandate notification within 72 hours of detection — that clock is already running), and reputational (customers are already raising tickets — damage has started, not just begun as a risk).

The 72-hour clock

Under GDPR and similar frameworks, the mandatory regulatory notification window starts at the point of detection, not at the point of impact. The moment the deletion was confirmed, the clock started — regardless of intent. Regulators don't ask whether it was accidental. They ask whether personal data became unavailable to its subjects. It did.

The Assessment
Formal risk evaluation
Asset
Customer data exports — 180GB accumulated over three years, stored in a production S3 bucket and made available to customers on demand.
Threat
Accidental destructive CLI command by a junior engineer with over-broad IAM permissions. Non-malicious — which raises likelihood rather than lowering it, because human error scales with access breadth.
Vulnerability 1
No least-privilege IAM controls — a junior engineer had permissions to execute destructive commands against production S3 resources when their role required no such access. Staging and production shared a single AWS account with no technical separation.
Vulnerability 2
No S3 versioning, replication, or automated backup on production buckets — data deleted is data gone permanently, with no recovery path and direct regulatory implications.
Likelihood
HIGHSingle AWS account for staging and production with no technical separation. No IAM guardrails on destructive actions. Reliability of the control depended entirely on human accuracy — not a technical enforcement mechanism.
Impact
HIGHPermanent data loss with no recovery path. Mandatory regulatory notification triggered within 72 hours of detection. Reputational damage already materialising — customers raising tickets before the incident was formally declared.

HIGHHigh likelihood × High impact. This risk existed in the environment before the incident occurred — it simply had not been identified or treated.

The Response
Immediate actions and structural fixes
Right now — incident response
01
Notify regulatory authorities 72hr clock running
The mandatory regulatory notification window started at the point of detection. Under GDPR and equivalent frameworks, this is not optional and does not depend on whether the loss was accidental. The clock is running — this action cannot wait for structural fixes to be planned.
02
Send targeted customer notifications
Affected customers need direct communication — not a public statement, but a targeted notification to impacted users explaining what happened and what steps are being taken. Customers are already raising tickets, meaning this communication is already overdue.
03
Review production resource access logs
Conduct a log review of all actions performed against production resources. Given that IAM controls were over-broad across the entire engineering team, this incident may not be isolated — other accidental operations may have occurred without being detected or reported.
Structural fixes — close the vulnerabilities
04
Implement least-privilege IAM controls
Junior engineers should have no IAM permissions to execute destructive commands against production resources. Access should be scoped strictly to what each role requires — staging access for staging work, production access only where explicitly justified and approved. Separate AWS accounts for staging and production is the most robust architectural fix.
05
Enable S3 versioning, replication, and automated backup
All production S3 buckets must have versioning enabled so deleted objects can be recovered. Cross-region replication provides an additional layer of resilience. Automated backup policies should be enforced at the account level — not left as optional configuration on individual buckets.
